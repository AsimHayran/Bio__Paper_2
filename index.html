Environment

Nitrogen Cycle

The nitrogen cycle describes how nitrogen is converted between its various chemical forms as it circulates among the atmosphere, terrestrial, and marine ecosystems.

Process Description
Nitrogen Fixation Nitrogen gas (N₂) from the air is converted into ammonia (NH₃) by nitrogen-fixing bacteria
Nitrification Ammonia (NH₃) is converted into nitrites (NO₂⁻) and then nitrates (NO₃⁻) by nitrifying bacteria
Assimilation Plants take in nitrates (NO₃⁻) from the soil and use them to make proteins and DNA
Ammonification When plants and animals die (or produce waste), decomposers break them down into ammonia (NH₃)
Denitrification Denitrifying bacteria convert nitrates (NO₃⁻) back into nitrogen gas (N₂), which returns to the air

Carbon Cycle

The carbon cycle is the biogeochemical cycle by which carbon is exchanged among the biosphere, pedosphere, geosphere, hydrosphere, and atmosphere of the Earth.

Process Description Equation
Photosynthesis Plants convert CO₂ and water into glucose and oxygen using sunlight CO₂ + H₂O → Glucose + O₂
Respiration Organisms break down glucose to release energy, producing CO₂ and water Glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + Energy
Feeding (Consumption) Animals eat plants, passing carbon through the food chain -
Decomposition When organisms die, decomposers break them down, releasing CO₂ or forming fossil fuels over millions of years -
Combustion (Burning) When fossil fuels or wood are burned, CO₂ is released into the air -
Fossilisation & Sedimentation Dead organisms get buried and compressed over millions of years, forming fossil fuels -

Animal Coordination

Hormones

Hormones are chemical messengers that are secreted directly into the blood, which carries them to organs and tissues of the body to exert their functions.

Hormone Produced By Target Organ Function
Insulin Pancreas Liver, muscles Regulates the blood glucose level
Adrenaline Adrenal glands Heart, muscles Prepares the body for a fight or flight response
Thyroxine Thyroid gland All body cells Regulates metabolic rate
Many hormones Pituitary gland Kidneys Produces and releases hormones that control other glands
Oestrogen Ovaries Uterus Involved in the menstrual cycle
Testosterone Testes Male reproductive organs Controls puberty and sperm production in males

Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle is a series of natural changes in hormone production and the structures of the uterus and ovaries of the female reproductive system that make pregnancy possible.

  • FSH - Matures the egg
  • LH - Releases the egg
  • Oestrogen - Builds-up the lining of the uterus
  • Progesterone - Maintains the lining thickness

Menstrual Cycle Stages (28 days)

Stage Day Process
Stage 1 Day 1 Pituitary gland releases FSH → causes ovaries to make oestrogen
Stage 2 Day 4 Oestrogen thickens the uterus lining and stops the pituitary gland
Stage 3 Day 14 Pituitary gland releases LH → LH releases eggs and ovaries release progesterone
Stage 4 Day 28 Progesterone keeps uterus lining thick and stops the pituitary gland from releasing FSH and LH

Controlling Fertility

Managing the ability to get pregnant. Either by increasing the chances of pregnancy or by preventing pregnancy.

IVF (In Vitro Fertilisation)

What is it: A fertility treatment where egg and sperm are combined outside the body in a lab.

Process:

  1. Woman is given hormones to stimulate multiple eggs
  2. Eggs are collected from ovaries
  3. Eggs are fertilised by sperm in the lab
  4. Fertilised embryos grow for a few days
  5. One or more embryos are implanted back into the woman's uterus to develop naturally

Purpose: Helps couples who have problems with fertility (e.g., blocked fallopian tubes, low sperm count)

Pros: Can enable pregnancy when natural conception is difficult

Cons: Expensive, not always successful, emotionally and physically demanding

Clomifene Therapy

What it is: A fertility drug that stimulates the pituitary gland to release more FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone)

How it works: Increased FSH causes ovaries to mature and release eggs (induces ovulation)

Purpose: Used for women who do not ovulate regularly or have low hormone levels affecting fertility

Pros: Non-invasive, can improve chances of natural conception

Cons: Possible side effects: hot flushes, headaches, multiple pregnancies (twins, triplets)

Transport in Animals

Blood Vessels

Vessel Type Structure Function Blood Pressure
Arteries
  • Thick, muscular, and elastic walls to handle high pressure
  • Narrow lumen to maintain pressure
  • No valves
 Carry oxygenated blood away from the heart to the rest of the body under pressure High pressure due to forceful heart contractions
Capillaries
  • One cell thick walls (very thin) to allow easy exchange
  • Narrow lumen, just wide enough for red blood cells to pass through single-file
 Allow exchange of gases, nutrients, and waste between blood and body cells Low pressure
Veins
  • Thinner walls than arteries
  • Larger lumen to help blood flow at low pressure
  • Valves present to prevent backflow of blood
 Carry deoxygenated blood back to the heart Low pressure compared to arteries

The Heart

The heart is divided into four chambers:

  1. Right Atrium – Receives deoxygenated blood from the body via the vena cava
  2. Right Ventricle – Pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery
  3. Left Atrium – Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs via the pulmonary veins
  4. Left Ventricle – Pumps oxygenated blood to the body via the aorta (has the thickest muscle wall for high-pressure pumping)

Key Functions of the Heart

  • Double Pump System
    • Right side pumps blood to the lungs (pulmonary circulation) for oxygenation
    • Left side pumps oxygen-rich blood to the body (systemic circulation)
  • Valves (tricuspid, bicuspid, pulmonary, aortic) prevent backflow of blood
  • Coronary Arteries supply the heart muscle itself with oxygenated blood

Steps of Blood Flow Through the Heart

  1. Deoxygenated Blood Enters the Heart

    Deoxygenated blood from the body flows into the right atrium through the vena cava

  2. Blood Moves to the Right Ventricle

    The right atrium contracts, pushing blood through the tricuspid valve into the right ventricle

  3. Blood is Pumped to the Lungs

    The right ventricle contracts, forcing blood through the pulmonary valve into the pulmonary artery. The blood travels to the lungs, where it picks up oxygen and releases carbon dioxide

  4. Oxygenated Blood Returns to the Heart

    Oxygenated blood returns from the lungs via the pulmonary veins

  5. Blood Moves to the Left Ventricle

    The left atrium contracts, pushing blood through the bicuspid valve into the left ventricle

  6. Blood is Pumped to the Body

    The left ventricle contracts strongly, forcing blood through the aortic valve into the aorta. The oxygen-rich blood is then distributed to the entire body through smaller arteries and capillaries

Blood Components

Blood is made up of four main components:

Component Description Function
Plasma (55%) A yellow liquid
  • Carries nutrients (e.g., glucose, amino acids)
  • Carries waste products (e.g., CO₂, urea)
  • Carries hormones
  • Carries proteins (e.g., antibodies, clotting factors)
Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes)
  • Contain haemoglobin to carry oxygen
  • No nucleus (more space for haemoglobin)
  • Biconcave shape (large surface area for gas exchange)
 Transport oxygen from lungs to tissues
White Blood Cells (Leucocytes)
  • Part of the immune system
  • Have a nucleus
  • Fight infections (some produce antibodies, others engulf pathogens via phagocytosis)
Platelets Tiny cell fragments Help clot blood (prevent excessive bleeding)

Functions of Blood

  1. Transport
    • Oxygen (from lungs to cells via red blood cells)
    • Carbon dioxide (from cells to lungs to be exhaled)
    • Nutrients (e.g., glucose from digestion to cells)
    • Waste (e.g., urea to kidneys for excretion)
    • Hormones (chemical messengers, e.g., insulin)
  2. Defence
    • White blood cells destroy pathogens (bacteria, viruses)
    • Antibodies help fight infections
  3. Temperature Regulation
    • Blood absorbs and distributes heat to maintain body temperature
  4. Clotting (Prevents Blood Loss)
    • Platelets form clots to seal wounds

Plant Structures

Stomata and Transpiration

Stomata

Tiny pores found mostly on the underside of plant leaves.

Structure:

  • Each stoma (singular) is surrounded by guard cells that control its opening and closing
  • Guard cells change shape to open or close the pore

Function:

  • Allow gas exchange between the plant and the atmosphere
  • Let carbon dioxide (CO₂) in for photosynthesis
  • Let oxygen (O₂) out as a byproduct of photosynthesis
  • Enable water vapor to exit during transpiration

Transpiration

The process by which water vapor is lost from a plant. Water moves from high concentration to low concentration.

Factors affecting transpiration:

  • Light intensity (more light → more transpiration)
  • Temperature (higher temp → faster transpiration)
  • Humidity (lower humidity → more transpiration)
  • Wind (more wind → more transpiration)

How it happens:

  1. Water is absorbed by roots from the soil
  2. Water travels up the plant through xylem vessels
  3. Water evaporates from the leaf surfaces inside the leaf
  4. Water vapor exits the leaf through open stomata into the air

Transport and Translocation

Translocation

The transport of minerals from leaves to other parts of the plant using active transport.

Why it happens: Transpiration creates a water potential gradient that pulls water up from the roots, helping transport nutrients and keeping cells turgid.

Active Transport: Movement of substances from a low concentration to a high concentration, against the concentration gradient, using energy.

Xylem and Phloem

Tissue Function Key Features Process
Xylem
  1. Transports water and dissolved minerals (e.g., nitrates) from roots to leaves (upwards only)
  2. Provides structural support (contains lignin, making plant stems rigid)
  1. Dead cells (hollow tubes with no cytoplasm)
  2. Lignin (a tough, waterproof substance) that strengthens the walls
  3. Transpiration stream (water movement driven by evaporation from leaves)
  1. Roots absorb water and minerals from soil
  2. Water moves up xylem vessels by transpiration pull (evaporation from leaves creates suction)
  3. Cohesion-tension theory – Water molecules stick together (cohesion) and are pulled upwards
Phloem
  1. Transports minerals to growing and storage tissues (up and down the plant). This process is called translocation
  1. Living cells (unlike xylem)
  2. Made of sieve tube elements (no nucleus, allows sap flow) and companion cells (provide energy for translocation)
  3. Requires energy (ATP) for active transport
  1. Sugars are made in source
  2. Sugars are actively loaded into phloem
  3. Water follows by osmosis, creating pressure that pushes sap to sink (e.g., roots, fruits)

Nervous System

Synapses - Connect Neurons

A synapse is the junction between two neurons or between a neuron and an effector cell (such as a muscle or gland cell).

Process of Synaptic Transmission:

  1. Electrical impulse reaches the end of the presynaptic neuron
  2. This triggers the release of neurotransmitter molecules from vesicles
  3. Neurotransmitters diffuse across the synaptic cleft
  4. Neurotransmitters bind to receptor molecules on the postsynaptic membrane
  5. This binding triggers a new electrical impulse in the postsynaptic neuron
  6. Neurotransmitters are then broken down by enzymes or reabsorbed

Reflex Arc

A reflex arc is the neural pathway that mediates a reflex action. It allows for rapid, involuntary responses to stimuli.

Components of a Reflex Arc:

  1. Receptor - Detects the stimulus
  2. Sensory Neuron - Carries impulse to CNS
  3. Relay Neuron - In spinal cord, connects sensory to motor neuron
  4. Motor Neuron - Carries impulse to effector
  5. Effector - Muscle or gland that responds

Example: When you touch something hot:

  1. Heat receptors in skin detect high temperature
  2. Sensory neuron sends impulse to spinal cord
  3. Relay neuron passes signal to motor neuron
  4. Motor neuron sends impulse to arm muscles
  5. Arm muscles contract to pull hand away

Neurons

Neurons are specialized cells that transmit electrical and chemical signals in the nervous system.

Neuron Type Structure Function
Sensory Neurons
  • Long dendrites
  • Short axon
  • Cell body in middle
 Carry impulses from receptors to CNS
Relay Neurons (Interneurons)
  • Many short dendrites
  • Short axon
 Connect sensory and motor neurons in CNS
Motor Neurons
  • Short dendrites
  • Long axon
  • Cell body at one end
 Carry impulses from CNS to effectors (muscles/glands)

Structure of a Typical Neuron

  • Dendrites - Receive signals from other neurons
  • Cell Body - Contains nucleus and organelles
  • Axon - Long fiber that carries impulses away from cell body
  • Myelin Sheath - Fatty layer that insulates axon and speeds up impulse
  • Nodes of Ranvier - Gaps in myelin sheath where impulses "jump"
  • Synaptic Terminals - End of axon that releases neurotransmitters
